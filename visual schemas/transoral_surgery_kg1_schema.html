KG1 Transoral Surgery Schema

A one-page visual summary of when transoral surgery is supported, should trigger caution, or should generally be avoided in the KG1 framework. This page combines the benchmark’s TLM and TORS logic into a bedside-style decision aid for multidisciplinary discussion.

Proceed / Favorable

Transoral surgery is aligned with KG1
  • Adequate laryngeal exposure on preoperative assessment is the first gate.
  • Glottic cT2 remains favorable when there is no blocking cartilage, posterior spread, or exposure problem.
  • Glottic cT3 can still be transoral when disease is anterior-limited and posterior structures are spared.
  • Supraglottic cT3 with limited pre-epiglottic space involvement supports TLM/TORS.
  • Older age alone does not remove transoral eligibility if the patient is otherwise fit.

Pause / Discuss

Borderline anatomy or site-dependent rules
  • Bulky unfavorable cT2 may still be transoral if exposure is good and disease stays anterior.
  • Anterior commissure involvement is acceptable only when exposure remains good.
  • Focal inner thyroid cartilage cortex blocks TLM specifically, but does not automatically block all larynx preservation.
  • Pronounced supraglottic PES shifts preference from TLM/TORS toward OPHL rather than transoral surgery.
  • Same sign, different meaning: thyroid cartilage erosion behaves differently in glottic versus supraglottic disease.

Avoid / Contraindicated

KG1 pushes away from transoral surgery
  • Insufficient laryngeal exposure is a hard stop for TLM and TORS.
  • Posterior paraglottic spread, massive PES abutting the membrane, and posterior subglottic extension block TLM.
  • Glottic thyroid cartilage erosion, outer cortex breach, or major cT4a extension remove transoral candidacy.
  • Bilateral arytenoid involvement, magic plane crossing, and major neurologic/functional impairment argue strongly against transoral conservation.
  • Hypopharyngeal primary is not a preferred transoral scenario in KG1.

Site-Specific Reading

Glottic cT2

Transoral surgery is supported when exposure is adequate and the tumor remains technically resectable.

Adequate exposure No cartilage involvement No posterior spread AC only if exposure is good

Glottic cT3

Fixed cord alone does not eliminate TLM. What matters is whether the anatomy stays anterior and exposure remains workable.

Anterior-limited PGS Posterior PGS Posterior subglottic disease Inner cortex focal involvement = no TLM

Supraglottic cT3

This is the main TORS-positive zone in KG1. Limited PES supports TLM/TORS; pronounced PES does not.

Limited PES Adequate exposure Pronounced PES -> prefer OPHL Site switch to hypopharynx

Hypopharyngeal / cT4a

KG1 consistently downgrades transoral surgery here. Once disease is hypopharyngeal or clearly cT4a with major extension, do not frame the case as a transoral problem.

Hypopharyngeal primary Outer cortex / full-thickness cartilage Extralaryngeal extension TLM excluded in selected cT4a

KG1 Rule Anchors

S5R Insufficient laryngeal exposure is a hard contraindication for transoral surgery.
S7R Posterior paraglottic disease, difficult anterior commissure exposure, posterior subglottic disease, and certain PES patterns block TLM.
S8 Anterior commissure involvement can remain transoral only when exposure is good.
S9R / S10R Thyroid cartilage erosion is site-conditional: glottic disease blocks TLM, while supraglottic disease may redirect toward OPHL rather than transoral surgery.
S12 / S45 Hypopharyngeal primary and cT4a extension remove transoral surgery from the preferred option set.
S6R / S80 Age by itself is not a transoral contraindication in a fit patient.

How to Use This Sheet in Clinic

  • Start with exposure and site before debating modality.
  • If the anatomy is anterior, limited, and reachable, transoral surgery stays on the table.
  • If the disease is posterior, cartilage-breaching, hypopharyngeal, or clearly cT4a, move the discussion away from transoral surgery early.
  • When a finding seems contradictory, ask whether the site has changed the rule.
