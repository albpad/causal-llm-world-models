in Large Language Models for Clinical Oncology Decision-Making

Abstract
Background: Clinical medicine is not practiced on averages but on individual patients whose decisive features often lie at the edge of the distribution. In that setting, benchmark success does not establish that a model encodes an intervention-sensitive causal representation of a clinical domain. The critical question is not only whether an LLM can reproduce a plausible recommendation, but whether it changes that recommendation in the correct causal direction when clinically relevant variables are perturbed.
Methods: We developed a behavioral causal discovery framework to recover and evaluate LLMs’ implicit causal graphs for head and neck oncology decision-making. Using 88 counterfactual clinical vignettes (12 baselines and 76 perturbations) derived from an international Delphi consensus, we operationalized 60 statement-linked treatment rules into a 58-edge gold-standard graph. Five open-access models were evaluated through the Together.ai API: Kimi K2.5, DeepSeek-R1, Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B-Instruct, Mistral-Small-24B-Instruct-2501, and Llama 3.1-8B. The primary analysis was harmonized to 15 runs per model (6,600 vignette-run combinations total) using a two-phase prompting protocol. Before locking the final study, we audited benchmark integrity and performed layered parser validation. Recovered graphs were evaluated primarily along Coverage, Fidelity, and Discriminability, with Stability used as an auxiliary reproducibility domain.
Results: Benchmark review confirmed 12 baselines, 76 perturbations, 60 statement-linked rules, 58 evaluation edges, zero missing source traceability among non-null perturbations, zero null-control drift, and zero staging inconsistencies. The parser showed near-ceiling performance on deterministic gold templates (98.9% exact match) and perfect performance on structured stance snippets (100%), whereas apparent low agreement on real outputs was driven predominantly by model disagreement with the gold standard, aggregate-label effects, and targeted query-space undercoverage rather than widespread parser failure. In the final five-model comparison, Kimi K2.5 achieved the broadest graph recovery (43 of 58 edges; soft recall, 74.1% [95% confidence interval {CI}, 62.1 to 84.5]; soft precision, 66.2%) and strongest causal-versus-noise separation (signal-to-noise ratio [SNR], 2.46 [95% CI, 1.80 to 3.23]), whereas DeepSeek-R1 showed the strongest fidelity-balanced profile (36 of 58 edges; soft-edge direction accuracy, 77.8% [95% CI, 63.2 to 90.2]; structural intervention distance [SID], 34 of 243 [14.0% wrong; 95% CI, 9.9 to 18.5]; veridical split-half agreement, 78.2%). Qwen3-Next-80B and Mistral-Small-24B showed partial but brittle structure. Llama 3.1-8B remained fragmentary (10 of 58 edges; soft recall, 17.2% [95% CI, 8.6 to 27.6]; SID, 45 of 233 [19.3% wrong; 95% CI, 14.2 to 24.5]; SNR, 0.80 [95% CI, 0.71 to 1.74]). Threshold-sensitivity analyses preserved the same qualitative ordering. No model achieved complete or expert-level causal graph recovery.
Conclusions: Behavioral causal discovery provides a practical externalist framework for evaluating whether LLMs encode clinically meaningful causal structure. Across five open-access models, frontier systems showed only partial and model-dependent world-model competence. For clinical deployment, benchmark-style success is insufficient: what matters is whether recommendations adapt in the correct causal direction when a case departs from the average pattern.

Introduction
Clinical medicine is not practiced on averages but on individual patients whose decisive features often lie at the edge of the distribution. The clinically consequential question is rarely what is usually done; it is what should change when one factor changes the treatment regime. In laryngeal cancer, these factors can include poor laryngeal exposure, aspiration risk, cisplatin ineligibility, or failure of induction chemotherapy. A clinically useful model must therefore reason causally about what makes a given case an exception, rather than merely reproduce the statistical center of prior cases. Craik’s original conception of cognition as the construction of “small-scale models” of reality for anticipation and simulation remains a useful starting point for framing this problem.1 Lake, Ullman, Tenenbaum, and Gershman later formalized the broader AI implication: systems that generalize robustly should be expected to encode structured causal models rather than isolated associations.2

Large language models (LLMs) now perform impressively on medical examinations, question-answering benchmarks, and many vignette-style evaluations.3-6 Yet those successes do not resolve a central question: do these systems carry an intervention-sensitive representation of the clinical world, or do they primarily reproduce plausible answers through statistical imitation of common patterns? This distinction is not semantic. It matters most when a model encounters novel combinations of variables, conflicting constraints, contraindications, or counterfactual cases in which a recommendation should change for a specific reason.7,8 In clinical practice, those edge cases are not peripheral; they are often where expert judgment matters.

Current evaluation paradigms are poorly matched to that question. Multiple-choice examinations mainly test factual recall.9 Agreement studies on static clinical vignettes measure concordance with experts at the level of final recommendation but cannot distinguish latent causal competence from memorised associations.10 Even chain-of-thought or free-text reasoning analyses describe what the model says, not the structure that governs how its outputs adapt under intervention.11 Recent work has sharpened this concern. Agrawal et al described an “evaluation illusion” in medicine, in which benchmark performance overstates real clinical capability.12 Griot et al showed that strong medical LLMs lack key metacognitive properties required for dependable reasoning.13 Kim et al demonstrated that clinical problem-solving errors frequently arise from inflexible reasoning rather than absent facts.14 Zhou et al and Bedi et al further showed that reasoning quality and reasoning fidelity are themselves measurable, and not reducible to final-answer accuracy.15,16

More broadly, the AI literature has renewed interest in whether LLMs exhibit causal or world-model-like structure.2,17,18 However, most available causal benchmarks test explicit textual inference, formal counterfactual question answering, or abstract reasoning over symbolic variables. They do not recover an expert-domain causal graph from a model’s behavioral shifts on realistic, high-dimensional decision problems. That is the methodological gap addressed here.

We introduce behavioral causal discovery: a framework for recovering an LLM’s implicit causal graph by applying controlled clinical perturbations to baseline cases and measuring how the model’s treatment recommendations change. The recovered graph (KG2) is then compared with an independently derived expert reference graph (KG1), here based on an international Delphi consensus on laryngeal preservation strategies.19 This design does not assume access to model weights, latent states, or internal activations. Instead, it evaluates the observable behavioral consequence of intervention.

We organize evaluation around three primary dimensions and one auxiliary reproducibility domain. Coverage asks how much of the expert graph is behaviorally recoverable. Fidelity asks whether recovered edges have the correct sign and interventional profile. Discriminability asks whether the model separates clinically meaningful perturbations from irrelevant changes. Stability asks whether the recovered structure is reproducible across run subsets without rewarding consistent emptiness or consistent wrongness. These dimensions are individually interpretable and jointly necessary. A model may recover many edges but with poor sign control, or may appear stable while being equally responsive to noise. The core claim of this study is therefore not that one model “wins” a benchmark, but that behavioral graph recovery provides a more rigorous way to test whether an LLM exhibits clinically meaningful causal structure in the settings where average-pattern imitation is most likely to fail.

Methods
Study Design and Reporting Standards
This was a computational evaluation study of implicit causal representations in LLMs using semi-synthetic counterfactual clinical vignettes. No patients were enrolled and no protected health information was used. The study was designed and reported with the MI-CLAIM-GEN checklist20 and the DEAL framework for LLM studies in medicine.21 The complete vignette battery, benchmark-audit outputs, parser-validation artifacts, raw model outputs, and final analysis files are included in the repository and supplementary materials.

Reference Causal Graph (KG1)
The gold-standard causal graph (KG1) was derived from the international Delphi consensus on laryngeal preservation strategies published in Lancet Oncology (Ferrari et al., 2025).19 From 137 consensus statements, we operationalised 60 statement-linked treatment rules into a final 58-edge graph linking clinically perturbed variables to treatment recommendations, contraindications, or conditional restrictions. Source variables spanned tumour staging and extent, surgical access, pretreatment function, patient fitness, chemotherapy eligibility, and post-induction chemotherapy response. Target nodes comprised 16 treatment categories including TLM, TORS, OPHL subtypes, total laryngectomy, radiotherapy-alone strategies, concurrent CRT, and induction chemotherapy pathways. Each edge retained its source statement ID, rule type, and consensus metadata.

Counterfactual Vignette Battery
We constructed 88 clinical vignettes organized into 12 baselines and 76 perturbations. The 12 baselines span 10 clinical families: glottic cT2, glottic cT3, supraglottic cT3, hypopharyngeal cancer, cT4a unselected, cT4a selected for larynx preservation, cisplatin eligibility, post-induction chemotherapy response, elderly/frail presentations, and pretreatment function. Each perturbation modified one clinically relevant variable from its parent baseline while holding all others fixed. The perturbations comprised 48 flip perturbations, 12 escalation perturbations, 12 null perturbations, 3 gray-zone perturbations, and 1 multi-variable perturbation.

Vignette generation was performed by a deterministic pipeline with staging-consistency checks and rule-level traceability back to the source Delphi statements. Before finalising the study, we manually reviewed the vignettes with an expert clinician and re-audited the full battery as the benchmark source of truth. This audit confirmed the expected structure of 12 baselines and 76 perturbations, zero missing source traceability among non-null items, zero null-control drift, and zero staging inconsistencies. The audit also identified a protocol limitation: some family-specific structured menus did not exhaustively query all concrete treatment labels represented in the battery. We therefore retained the collected dataset, kept aggregate labels deterministic rather than free-floating, and treated residual query-space undercoverage as an explicit limitation rather than silently redefining the benchmark.

Model Selection and Access
Five models were selected to span distinct capability regimes. Kimi K2.5 and DeepSeek-R1 represented large reasoning-oriented systems. Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B-Instruct provided a large frontier instruct comparator. Mistral-Small-24B-Instruct-2501 represented a capable mid-size model. Llama 3.1-8B served as the low-capacity baseline. All models were accessed through the Together.ai chat-completions API without retrieval augmentation, fine-tuning, or exemplar prompting. This zero-shot design targeted intrinsic causal structure rather than externally injected knowledge.

The primary reported dataset was harmonised to 15 runs per model using run indices 0-14. This yielded 1,320 vignette-run combinations per model and 6,600 total combinations across five models. Each combination consisted of two API calls, one per prompting phase, for 13,200 calls in the harmonised dataset. Earlier exploratory and recovery outputs were preserved for provenance but were excluded from the final canonical comparison.

Prompting Protocol
Each vignette was evaluated in two phases within a single conversation. Phase 1 elicited an open-ended treatment recommendation and rationale from the full vignette text. Phase 2 appended the Phase 1 response and asked family-specific treatment questions requiring one of four explicit stance labels: APPROPRIATE, CONTRAINDICATED, RELATIVELY CONTRAINDICATED, or UNCERTAIN. The structured phase increased comparability and enabled high-confidence parser extraction; the open-ended phase preserved coverage for treatments not explicitly present in the family-specific menu. This design became especially important after the benchmark audit showed that the family-specific menus were not fully exhaustive for all concrete labels.

Response Parsing and Layered Validation
Responses were parsed using a deterministic rule-based NLP pipeline that detects treatment mentions, assigns one of four stance labels, reconciles Phase 1 and Phase 2 evidence, and derives aggregate labels (`ophl_any`, `surgical_lp`, `nonsurgical_lp`) from concrete treatment stances rather than treating them as independent parser targets.

Because low agreement between parsed outputs and benchmark expectations can reflect either parser defects or genuine model errors, parser review was performed in three layers. First, the parser was applied to deterministic gold-response templates generated directly from the battery. Second, it was applied to structured stance snippets with known labels, directly testing the high-confidence Phase 2 path. Third, real-output mismatches were audited and categorised as query-space gaps, aggregate-label mismatches, model omissions, candidate parser misses, or model-versus-gold stance disagreements. This layered validation allowed us to separate true parser failure from disagreement driven by model behaviour or protocol structure.

Causal Graph Recovery
The recovered behavioral graph (KG2) was constructed from the model’s response to controlled clinical perturbations rather than specified a priori. Each perturbation modified one clinically relevant variable from its parent baseline while holding the remainder of the vignette constant. For each perturbation-treatment-model combination, model outputs were parsed into one of four stance labels (APPROPRIATE, CONTRAINDICATED, RELATIVELY CONTRAINDICATED, or UNCERTAIN), thereby defining baseline and perturbed stance distributions across repeated runs. A candidate behavioral edge was inferred when changing the source variable produced a reproducible shift in stance toward a given treatment.

Edge evidence was quantified by the Jensen-Shannon divergence (JSD) between baseline and perturbed stance distributions and by Fisher’s exact test on recommendation-versus-not-recommendation counts, with Benjamini-Hochberg correction at q = 0.05. The direction of each candidate edge was defined by the sign of the change in recommendation probability relative to the expert-expected effect of the perturbation. Because multiple perturbations could probe the same expert rule in different contexts, probe-level results were aggregated to the edge level. In the locked primary analysis, hard detection required that a majority of an edge’s probes be significant; soft detection required either an omnibus distribution shift or a probe-level detection rate of at least 0.25. KG2 therefore represents a model-specific causal-behavioral graph recovered from intervention, with edges defined by reproducible treatment adaptation when the clinical state changed. Because thresholded graph recovery can depend on detection cut points, we also performed supplementary sensitivity analyses over soft detection-rate thresholds of 0.15 to 0.35 and hard thresholds spanning detection rates of 0.40 to 0.60 crossed with mean-JSD cut points of 0.05 to 0.15.

Three-Dimensional Evaluation Framework
Coverage was defined as behaviorally recoverable graph breadth and was reported with soft recall as the primary measure, together with soft precision and soft false discovery rate (FDR) to distinguish broad recovery from noisy over-coverage. Hard recall and hard precision were retained only as secondary sensitivity checks. Fidelity was measured with three complementary views: soft-edge direction accuracy as the primary directional readout, hard-edge direction accuracy as a stricter secondary directional check, and Structural Intervention Distance (SID), adapted from Peters and Buhlmann22 to count intervention-treatment mismatches across the full perturbation battery. Soft-edge direction accuracy was defined as the fraction of soft-detected edges whose observed shift matched the expected direction. We treated the soft-edge view as primary because it reflects directional behavior across the recovered graph, whereas the hard-edge view depends on a much smaller subset and can collapse to misleading extremes in sparse models. Supplementary fidelity analyses also included clinically weighted wrong-direction and SID rates. Treatment-specific severity weights were assigned a priori: tier 3 (weight 3) for any error involving total laryngectomy; tier 2 (weight 2) for organ-preservation regime shifts involving transoral surgery, OPHL variants, and non-surgical larynx-preservation pathways; and tier 1 (weight 1) for lower-stakes systemic-fallback refinements. Weighted wrong-direction and weighted SID rates were then computed as weight-normalized error burdens across the corresponding edge and intervention sets. Discriminability measured the separation between clinically meaningful and irrelevant perturbations. Its primary metrics were the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), defined as mean causal JSD divided by mean null JSD, and detection power, defined as the fraction of causal JSD values exceeding the 95th percentile of each model’s null distribution. Stability was assessed with split-half agreement and related reproducibility analyses, but interpreted only in the context of coverage and veridical edge recovery to avoid over-rewarding sparse graphs. No single composite index was used for primary interpretation.

Statistical Analysis
All significance tests were two-sided with alpha = 0.05, with Benjamini-Hochberg correction at q = 0.05 within each model. Edge-level JSD confidence intervals used bias-corrected and accelerated resampling with 2,000 replicates and Dirichlet smoothing. Study-level confidence intervals for coverage, fidelity, and discriminability metrics used nonparametric percentile bootstrap with 2,000 replicates and a fixed seed, with bootstrap units chosen according to the metric: edge-level records for coverage and direction metrics, intervention-treatment pairs for SID, and causal and null JSD tests for SNR and detection power. Supplementary analyses comprised the threshold-sensitivity sweep, a family-stratified analysis across the 10 clinical families, and clinically weighted fidelity analyses. The full analysis stack was implemented in Python 3.10 and executed deterministically given identical inputs.

Results
Benchmark and Parser Lock
The benchmark audit confirmed that the final study battery was internally coherent and source-traceable: 12 baselines, 76 perturbations, 88 total items, 60 statement-linked treatment rules, and 58 evaluation edges. No non-null perturbation lacked a source anchor, no null perturbation drifted from its parent baseline expectations, and no staging inconsistency was detected in the generated cases. The remaining benchmark-level issue was not source correctness but protocol coverage: several family-specific structured menus under-probed concrete labels such as OPHL subtypes, TLM in selected advanced-stage contexts, and some cisplatin-fallback regimens. We therefore interpret the recovered graphs in the context of a clinically valid but not perfectly exhaustive structured question space.

The parser lock showed a similar pattern. On deterministic gold templates, the parser achieved 98.9% exact match, with 100% precision and recall for both recommended and excluded labels on the gold targets. On structured stance snippets, it achieved 100% exact match across all four stance labels. Mismatch decomposition showed that only 551 of 32,856 classified mismatches (1.7%) were candidate parser misses, whereas most reflected model-versus-gold stance disagreement, aggregate-label abstraction differences, or structured query-space undercoverage. Accordingly, the parser was reliable for extracting model outputs, and most residual discordance arose from model behavior or protocol limitations rather than widespread parsing error.

Coverage of Expert Causal Structure
Coverage varied widely across models (Table 1; Figure 4). Kimi K2.5 recovered 43 of 58 gold-standard edges at the soft threshold (soft recall, 74.1% [95% CI, 62.1 to 84.5]) with soft precision 66.2% (95% CI, 56.2 to 76.3) and soft FDR 33.8% (95% CI, 23.7 to 43.8). DeepSeek-R1 recovered 36 of 58 edges (soft recall, 62.1% [95% CI, 50.0 to 74.1]) with soft precision 60.0% (95% CI, 50.0 to 70.6) and soft FDR 40.0% (95% CI, 29.4 to 50.0). Mistral-Small-24B and Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B-Instruct occupied an intermediate range, recovering 25 of 58 (43.1% [95% CI, 31.0 to 55.2]) and 23 of 58 (39.7% [95% CI, 27.6 to 53.4]) edges, respectively. Llama 3.1-8B remained sparse, recovering only 10 of 58 edges (17.2% [95% CI, 8.6 to 27.6]).

These coverage differences were not merely quantitative. Kimi’s graph was the broadest but carried a material over-coverage burden, whereas DeepSeek-R1 recovered a smaller graph with cleaner directional and high-confidence structure. The sparse models highlight why recall alone is inadequate. Llama’s soft precision was superficially high (71.4%), but that reflected a small denominator rather than useful graph breadth; in practical terms, it missed most clinically important edges. Coverage therefore has to be read jointly as breadth, precision, and FDR.

Threshold-sensitivity analyses did not alter the qualitative coverage story (Supplementary Figure S1). Across all soft-threshold settings, Kimi retained the highest soft recall. Across the hard-threshold grid, Kimi retained the highest hard recall in most configurations, while DeepSeek-R1 preserved the most favorable balance between graph breadth and directional control. Llama remained the weakest coverage model throughout the sweep.

Fidelity of Recovered Structure
Fidelity separated the models in a different way from coverage (Table 1, Table 2, and Figure 3). DeepSeek-R1 had the strongest overall fidelity profile, combining the highest soft-edge direction accuracy (77.8% [95% CI, 63.2 to 90.2]) with SID 34 of 243 (14.0% wrong [95% CI, 9.9 to 18.5]). Kimi K2.5 showed broader graph recovery but lower sign control, with soft-edge direction accuracy 67.4% (95% CI, 53.1 to 80.0) and SID 41 of 246 (16.7% wrong [95% CI, 12.2 to 21.5]). Qwen3-Next-80B and Mistral-Small-24B showed reasonable soft-edge direction accuracy (73.9% [95% CI, 55.0 to 91.3] and 72.0% [95% CI, 53.6 to 88.9], respectively) alongside relatively favorable SID rates (13.5% and 13.1%). However, both models still recovered little hard-confidence structure; their hard-edge direction accuracy collapsed to 0% because the strict denominator was extremely small. That is precisely why soft-edge direction accuracy is the primary directional readout in this study and the hard-edge version only a secondary sensitivity check. Llama 3.1-8B remained weakest overall, with SID 45 of 233 (19.3% wrong [95% CI, 14.2 to 24.5]), soft-edge direction accuracy 50.0% (95% CI, 16.7 to 83.3), and no hard-detected directionally correct edges.

Risk-weighted fidelity analysis, which assigned the highest penalty to total-laryngectomy-related errors, preserved the same broad ordering while sharpening the clinical contrast (Table 2). DeepSeek-R1 had the lowest weighted wrong-direction rate (21.9%) and the lowest weighted SID rate (13.8%). Kimi K2.5 remained clearly broader but riskier (weighted wrong-direction rate, 31.3%; weighted SID rate, 15.9%), and Llama 3.1-8B worsened most once high-stakes errors were emphasized (weighted wrong-direction rate, 44.4%; weighted SID rate, 17.4%). These results matter clinically because total laryngectomy errors are not symmetric nuisances: inappropriate escalation sacrifices organ preservation, whereas failure to recommend total laryngectomy when required can compromise oncologic control.

This pattern matters because coverage alone would favor Kimi, whereas fidelity alone would overstate the strength of models that recover relatively little structure. Taken together, the results point to different regimes of causal competence rather than a single ranking axis.

Edge-Level Signal Strength
Edge-level JSD analyses showed a clear stratification in causal response magnitude (Figure 2; Table 2). Kimi K2.5 had the strongest mean causal signal (mean causal JSD, 0.187), followed by DeepSeek-R1 (0.154), Mistral-Small-24B (0.116), Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B-Instruct (0.103), and Llama 3.1-8B (0.059). Mean null JSD remained lower for Kimi (0.076), DeepSeek-R1 (0.072), Mistral-Small-24B (0.070), and Qwen3-Next-80B (0.055), but not for Llama, whose mean null JSD (0.074) exceeded its mean causal JSD.

At the edge level, this implies that the frontier models were not only detecting more edges but also moving more decisively on clinically relevant perturbations. Llama, by contrast, remained close to or below its own behavioral noise floor.

Discriminability: Signal versus Noise
Discriminability reinforced this separation (Table 1, Table 2, and Figure 4). Kimi K2.5 achieved the highest SNR (2.46 [95% CI, 1.80 to 3.23]) and the highest detection power (20.7% [95% CI, 12.7 to 29.9]), indicating the strongest separation between clinically meaningful perturbations and null controls. DeepSeek-R1 followed closely (SNR, 2.14 [95% CI, 1.41 to 2.80]; detection power, 17.3% [95% CI, 3.5 to 29.3]), and Qwen3-Next-80B and Mistral-Small-24B occupied an intermediate band (SNR, 1.87 and 1.66, respectively; detection power, 15.6% and 13.1%). Llama 3.1-8B was qualitatively distinct, with SNR 0.80 (95% CI, 0.71 to 1.74) and detection power 2.6% (95% CI, 0.0 to 25.6), meaning that null perturbations still elicited shifts comparable to or larger than causal perturbations.

This was the clearest empirical signature of an inadequate world model. Even when Llama produced superficially plausible recommendations, it did not reliably discriminate signal from irrelevance at the graph level.

Integrated Model Profiles
Taken together, the primary dimensions revealed five distinct behavioral profiles (Figure 1, Figure 3, and Table 3). Kimi K2.5 was broad but noisy: it recovered the largest share of the graph and best separated signal from null perturbations, but with more directional brittleness and more clinically weighted error burden than DeepSeek-R1. DeepSeek-R1 was the most balanced model: lower coverage than Kimi, but stronger sign control, lower weighted directional burden, and better coverage-conditioned stability without pathological responsiveness to noise. Qwen3-Next-80B and Mistral-Small-24B exhibited partial but brittle world-model structure: neither was graph-empty, but both remained too narrow at the strict threshold and too family-dependent to support broad clinical trust. Llama 3.1-8B remained fragmentary. Its apparently favorable conditional precision and raw stability were artifacts of a sparse graph and therefore did not imply useful clinical reliability.

Family-stratified analyses underscored the heterogeneity of these regimes. Kimi and DeepSeek-R1 were strongest in cisplatin-eligibility and post-induction-chemotherapy families, while glottic and supraglottic families exposed the brittleness of Qwen3-Next and Mistral-Small. No model was uniformly strong across all clinically relevant families, and the `cT4a_unselected` family remained baseline/null-control context rather than a directional family denominator.

Discussion
This study advances LLM evaluation in two linked ways. Methodologically, it introduces behavioral causal discovery as a practical way to recover an expert-domain behavioral graph from model outputs under controlled intervention. Empirically, it shows that current open-access LLMs do not exhibit complete or clinically reliable world models in this domain, even when evaluated under repeated probing, threshold-sensitivity analysis, and structured follow-up. Instead, they occupy graded regimes of partial causal competence. That distinction matters clinically because medicine is not organized around average-case pattern matching; it is organized around the exceptions, thresholds, contraindications, and reversals that change what should be done for a specific patient.

This distinction is important because it clarifies what benchmark success can and cannot show. A model may reproduce guideline-compatible answers on standard tasks while failing to recover the graph structure needed to adapt recommendations appropriately under intervention. That concern is increasingly reflected in recent medical-AI literature.12-16 Our findings extend it by showing that the problem is not only one of answer quality or metacognitive reliability, but of incomplete and model-dependent behavioral structure. Benchmark accuracy and articulated reasoning remain compatible with graph-level inadequacy. Static benchmark accuracy is therefore an insufficient surrogate for clinical readiness: the clinically relevant test is whether recommendations update with the correct causal direction when the case changes.

The five-model comparison also suggests that frontier progress is not one-dimensional. Kimi K2.5 showed the broadest behavioral graph and the strongest causal-versus-noise separation. DeepSeek-R1 recovered a somewhat smaller graph but with cleaner directional fidelity, lower clinically weighted error burden, and stronger overall balance across coverage, fidelity, discriminability, and auxiliary stability. Qwen3-Next-80B and Mistral-Small-24B demonstrated that meaningful causal structure can emerge below the frontier tier, but their graphs remained partial, family-dependent, and directionally brittle at stricter thresholds. Llama 3.1-8B served as an informative lower bound: it was not entirely unresponsive, but its low coverage and SNR below 1.0 indicate a system that cannot reliably distinguish causal signal from irrelevant perturbation.

These profiles also suggest a clinically important asymmetry: partial world-model competence may be more clinically misleading than obvious failure. A graph-empty model is easier to identify as non-deployable. A partially competent model can be locally correct often enough to invite trust while remaining brittle in the edge cases that reverse treatment logic. In that setting, apparent sophistication may increase the risk of over-reliance.

These results support a more disciplined operational use of the term “world model.” We do not claim to prove or disprove the existence of rich internal simulations in a philosophical sense. Rather, we define world-model competence behaviorally: if a model encodes clinically meaningful causal structure, controlled perturbations should recover a graph that is broad enough, directionally faithful enough, and sufficiently separated from noise to support reliable intervention-sensitive reasoning. The upgraded evaluation also clarifies that clinical risk is not monolithic. Missing edges imply under-adaptation and blind spots. Wrong-direction edges imply unsafe directional updating. Noise sensitivity implies false adaptation to irrelevant changes. These are distinct deployment risks, and a single scalar cannot summarize them without distortion. On that operational definition, the evaluated models show partial and heterogeneous world-model competence, not expert-grade causal mastery. For the broader world-model agenda, this matters because it offers an externalist test of causal structure: not whether a model can talk about causality, but whether intervention recovers a coherent behavioral graph.

Clinical Implications
For clinical AI, the immediate implication is that static benchmark success should not be treated as adequate evidence for deployment in decision support. A model may look competent in familiar cases yet remain causally brittle when exposed to contraindications, gray zones, or convergent risk factors. In that setting, human oversight may not fully mitigate the risk. If the model’s causal structure is unreliable, the clinician must effectively reconstruct the treatment logic from first principles rather than verify a dependable recommendation. The system may then function less as decision support and more as a source of anchoring or misplaced confidence.

The practical value of the present framework lies in its ability to decompose failure into actionable components. Coverage identifies missing causal pathways. Fidelity identifies wrong-direction or no-shift errors. Discriminability identifies models whose outputs are insufficiently separated from behavioral noise. These are different failure modes with different remediation strategies. Clinically, a wrong-direction update may be more consequential than silence, because it preserves the appearance of structured reasoning while moving the recommendation in the wrong direction. In the specific example of laryngeal cancer, the risk-weighted analysis further shows that total-laryngectomy-related mistakes deserve special attention: they are the clearest examples of errors whose clinical cost is not well captured by unweighted accuracy alone.

The framework also offers a way to target improvement. Edge-level failures and SID decompositions can guide retrieval augmentation, structured knowledge injection, family-specific prompting, or fine-tuning on clinically consequential perturbation pairs rather than on undifferentiated answer corpora. More broadly, the same externalist evaluation logic can be applied beyond oncology wherever a consensus- or guideline-derived causal structure can be operationalized as a perturbation battery.

Limitations
This study has several limitations. First, it evaluates one domain: head and neck oncology. While this represents a useful edge case, the generalizability of the findings to other medical specialties and non-medical expert domains requires direct testing. Second, the benchmark audit identified family-specific query-space undercoverage for some concrete labels, meaning that the structured menus did not exhaustively probe every treatment label represented in the battery. We mitigated this by retaining Phase 1 open-ended elicitation and deterministic aggregate-label derivation, but the remaining undercoverage should still temper interpretation of some per-label results. Third, the evaluation is behavioral rather than mechanistic; it infers latent structure from repeated output shifts rather than from internal activations or weight-space analyses. Fourth, the models differ in size, training data, architecture, and reasoning scaffolding, so the observed ordering should not be attributed to any single design variable.

Future Directions
Several extensions follow directly from these results. The first is external validation in additional domains, especially domains with clearer procedural counterfactuals or richer longitudinal structure. The second is intervention on the models themselves: retrieval-augmented, instruction-tuned, or knowledge-injected variants should be tested to determine whether missing edges reflect absent latent structure or merely inaccessible latent structure. A related architectural direction is to design systems that construct explicit causal state representations or graph-structured world models during reasoning, rather than relying only on latent text-space associations. The third is benchmark evolution. Future batteries should make the family-specific structured menus exhaustive wherever possible and should support larger real-output parser audits on the highest-ambiguity treatment families. Finally, the framework can be extended beyond pairwise perturbation testing to longer-horizon sequential planning settings, where a clinically meaningful world model should support counterfactual adaptation over multiple dependent steps.

Conclusions
Across five open-access models, no LLM exhibited a complete or clinically reliable world model for specialized head and neck oncology decision-making. Kimi K2.5 showed the broadest graph recovery and strongest causal-versus-noise separation. DeepSeek-R1 showed the strongest overall balance of coverage and fidelity. Qwen3-Next-80B and Mistral-Small-24B occupied an intermediate regime of partial but brittle causal structure. Llama 3.1-8B remained fragmentary and insufficiently discriminative. Because treatment decisions hinge on case-specific exceptions rather than average patterns, a clinically useful model must adapt recommendations in the correct causal direction when the case changes. Behavioral causal discovery, coupled with the three-dimensional framework of Coverage, Fidelity, and Discriminability, provides a more clinically meaningful and scientifically defensible basis for evaluating that capability than benchmark accuracy alone.

Appendix: Representative Vignette Families
The final supplementary package contains the full vignette battery, the KG1 traceability matrix, the benchmark-audit report, the parser-validation report, and the per-model parsed outputs for the complete five-model dataset. This appendix highlights four representative vignette families chosen to illustrate different aspects of the behavioral causal discovery framework.

1. Access-dependent transoral surgery (A1-P1)
Baseline case A1-BASE represents favourable cT2 glottic disease with adequate laryngeal exposure. Perturbation A1-P1 switches only laryngeal exposure from adequate to insufficient. The gold-standard expectation is that TLM should become inappropriate while non-transoral options remain available. This vignette family tests whether the recovered graph can represent a local access constraint rather than a global treatment collapse.

2. Global failure of conservative surgery (B1-P8)
Perturbation B1-P8 introduces bilateral arytenoid fixation into a cT3 glottic baseline. The gold-standard consequence is a shift away from conservative surgery toward total laryngectomy and non-surgical organ-preservation alternatives. This family probes whether the model can recognise a structural threshold crossing rather than a small preference adjustment.

3. Post-induction chemotherapy response (H1-P3 and H1-P4)
The H1 family evaluates whether the model updates treatment strategy after induction chemotherapy according to response category. Stable disease and progression should move the graph toward total laryngectomy and away from radiotherapy-alone strategies. These cases are useful because they test intervention-sensitive adaptation over time rather than a static baseline recommendation.

4. Systemic-treatment ineligibility and fallback structure (G1-ABS11)
Perturbation G1-ABS11 probes the fallback regime when cisplatin and other systemic intensification options are no longer viable. This family is informative because it concentrates several of the benchmark’s query-space limitations and because it exposes clinically important disagreements about fallback non-surgical larynx-preservation strategies.
