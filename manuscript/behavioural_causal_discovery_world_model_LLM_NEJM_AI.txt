Abstract
Background: Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly evaluated for clinical reasoning, yet current benchmarks primarily assess factual recall through multiple-choice questions. These evaluations cannot determine whether an LLM possesses a causal world model—an internal representation of how clinical variables interact to determine treatment outcomes—or merely pattern-matches from training data.
Methods: We developed a behavioural causal discovery framework to recover and evaluate LLMs’ implicit causal graphs for head and neck cancer treatment decisions. Using 88 counterfactual clinical vignettes (12 baselines, 76 perturbations) derived from an international Delphi consensus of 137 expert statements, we operationalised a 58-edge gold-standard causal graph and probed five models—Kimi K2.5, DeepSeek-R1, Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B-Instruct, Mistral-Small-24B-Instruct-2501, and Llama 3.1-8B—through the Together.ai API. The primary comparison was harmonised to 15 runs per model (6,600 vignette–run combinations total) using a two-phase prompting protocol with automated response parsing. Treatment stance distributions were compared between baseline–perturbation pairs using Fisher’s exact tests with Benjamini–Hochberg correction and Jensen–Shannon divergence. Recovered graphs were evaluated along three dimensions: Coverage, Fidelity, and Discriminability.
Results: Kimi K2.5 achieved the highest coverage (43/58 edges; 74.1% soft recall) and strongest discriminability (SNR 2.46), whereas DeepSeek-R1 showed the strongest overall balance of coverage and fidelity (36/58 edges; 62.1% soft recall, 66.7% direction accuracy, SID 34/243 [14.0%], WMI 0.634). Qwen3-Next-80B and Mistral-Small-24B were intermediate, recovering 39.7% and 43.1% of edges respectively with SNRs of 1.87 and 1.66. Llama 3.1-8B remained weakest (10/58 edges; 17.2% soft recall, SID 45/233 [19.3%], SNR 0.80). No model achieved complete or highly reliable expert-level causal graph recovery.
Conclusions: Behavioural causal discovery through counterfactual perturbation provides a rigorous, graph-theoretic framework for evaluating whether LLMs possess the causal understanding necessary for clinical deployment. The three-dimensional evaluation—Coverage, Fidelity, and Discriminability—offers complementary and independently interpretable diagnostics grounded in causal graph comparison metrics. Benchmark accuracy without causal evaluation is insufficient evidence for clinical readiness.
Introduction
Clinical decision-making requires more than factual knowledge; it demands a causal understanding of how patient characteristics, tumour biology, and treatment modalities interact to determine outcomes. Craik’s foundational insight that cognition builds “small-scale models” of reality for anticipation and simulation (1943) remains central to the cognitive sciences.1 Lake, Ullman, Tenenbaum, and Gershman formalised this into the requirement that intelligent systems build causal generative models enabling few-shot generalisation and robust transfer.2
Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive performance on medical examinations and clinical vignettes,3–5 yet a fundamental question remains unanswered: do these models actually understand the causal structure of clinical domains, or do they produce correct-seeming outputs through sophisticated pattern matching? This question is not merely academic. A model that pattern-matches correctly on common presentations but lacks causal understanding will fail unpredictably on novel combinations of clinical variables—precisely the scenarios where clinical decision support is most needed.6,7
Existing evaluation paradigms cannot answer this question. Multiple-choice benchmarks test factual recall.8 Clinical vignette studies measure agreement with expert panels but cannot distinguish genuine causal reasoning from memorised associations.9 Even chain-of-thought analyses reveal the model’s articulated reasoning, not its implicit causal representations.10
We address this gap by introducing behavioural causal discovery: a method for recovering an LLM’s implicit causal graph through systematic counterfactual perturbation. By presenting clinically grounded vignettes in which single variables are altered and observing the resulting treatment recommendation shifts, we reconstruct the model’s internal causal topology without requiring access to model weights or activations. The recovered graph is then evaluated against an independently derived expert consensus—the international Delphi agreement on laryngeal preservation strategies.11
We evaluate the recovered graphs along three complementary dimensions that together characterise the quality of the model’s world model: Coverage (how many gold-standard causal edges are recovered), Fidelity (structural accuracy of recovered edges, assessed through direction accuracy and the Structural Intervention Distance), and Discriminability (the model’s ability to distinguish causally meaningful perturbations from irrelevant noise, quantified as the signal-to-noise ratio). These dimensions are grounded in established causal graph comparison metrics and are independently interpretable, each addressing a distinct aspect of causal competence.
Methods
Study Design and Reporting Standards
This is a computational evaluation study that assesses the implicit causal representations of large language models through behavioural probing with counterfactual clinical vignettes. No patients were enrolled, and no protected health information was used; all clinical scenarios are semi-synthetic, constructed from published consensus parameters. The study was designed and reported following the Minimum Information about Clinical Artificial Intelligence Modeling for Generative Models (MI-CLAIM-GEN) checklist,21 which extends the original MI-CLAIM framework22 to address the specific requirements of generative model evaluation, including zero-shot prompting, open-ended output assessment, and reproducibility of stochastic outputs. In addition, we followed the DEAL-B (Development, Evaluation, and Assessment of Large Language Models) checklist,15 a reporting framework specifically designed for LLM studies in medicine. All analysis code, the vignette battery, prompting templates, and raw model outputs are available in the supplementary materials.
Reference Causal Graph (KG₁)
The gold-standard causal graph (designated KG₁) was derived from an international Delphi consensus on laryngeal preservation strategies published in Lancet Oncology (Ferrari et al., 2025).11 The consensus comprised 137 statements covering treatment selection criteria for head and neck squamous cell carcinoma, achieved through iterative Delphi rounds with international experts from multiple centres across Europe, North America, and Asia-Pacific. From these 137 statements, we operationalised 60 statement-linked treatment rules into a 58-edge causal graph linking clinically perturbed variables (source nodes) to treatment recommendations or contraindications (target nodes). Source variables span six clinical domains: tumour staging and extent (T stage, N stage, subsite, cartilage involvement, paraglottic and pre-epiglottic space invasion), patient fitness (ECOG performance status, age, comorbidities, CGA fitness classification), functional status (laryngeal function, swallowing, airway patency), surgical access (laryngeal exposure adequacy), chemotherapy eligibility (renal function, hearing, neuropathy, cardiac status), and treatment response (post-induction chemotherapy response category). Target nodes comprise 16 treatment modalities including transoral laser microsurgery (TLM), transoral robotic surgery (TORS), open partial horizontal laryngectomies (OPHL types I, II, IIB, III), total laryngectomy (TL), radiotherapy alone (RT), accelerated radiotherapy, concurrent chemoradiotherapy (CRT), induction chemotherapy followed by response-adapted treatment (ICT), high-dose cisplatin, cetuximab, and carboplatin-5FU. Each edge carries a consensus-derived type classification (absolute contraindication, relative contraindication, positive indication, or conditional recommendation) and a weight reflecting the Delphi agreement percentage.
Counterfactual Vignette Battery
We constructed 88 clinical vignettes organised into 12 baseline cases and 76 perturbations. The 12 baselines span 10 clinical families covering the major presentation patterns in laryngeal and hypopharyngeal cancer: glottic cT2 (2 baselines: favourable TLM-eligible, unfavourable), glottic cT3, supraglottic cT3, hypopharyngeal, cT4a unselected, cT4a selected for larynx preservation, cisplatin eligibility, post-induction chemotherapy response (2 baselines: complete response, stable disease), elderly/frail, and pretreatment function. Each baseline represents a clinically coherent patient scenario with complete demographic data, tumour staging, imaging findings, functional assessments, and laboratory values (vignette length: 721–1,095 characters, mean 888).
Each perturbation modifies exactly one clinical variable from its parent baseline while holding all others constant, implementing Pearl’s do-operator12 at the behavioural level. The 76 perturbations comprise five types: 48 flip perturbations (binary variable reversal, e.g., vocal cord mobile → fixed), 12 escalation perturbations (ordinal variable increase, e.g., limited paraglottic invasion → massive invasion), 3 grey-zone perturbations (entering clinical equipoise where the expert consensus is divided), 1 multi-variable perturbation (convergent contraindications), and 12 null perturbations. The null perturbations modify clinically irrelevant variables (patient name, hospital location, treating physician) that should produce no change in treatment recommendations, serving as negative controls for estimating each model’s behavioural noise floor. The battery operationalises 60 statement-linked treatment rules spanning the final 58-edge evaluation graph, with some rules probed through multiple perturbation contexts.
Vignette generation was automated using a deterministic pipeline (vignette_generator.py) that applies perturbation logic to baseline templates with built-in staging consistency checks. The staging propagation module enforces clinical coherence: for example, if a perturbation sets thyroid cartilage outer cortex invasion to present, the T stage is automatically propagated to cT4a. The generator also flags implausible combinations (e.g., bilateral arytenoid fixation with functional larynx, ECOG 0 with tracheostomy plus feeding tube). All 88 vignettes were reviewed by a senior head and neck surgical oncologist for clinical face validity before deployment.
Model Selection and Access
Five models were selected to span reasoning-centric, frontier instruct, mid-size instruct, and small baseline regimes. Kimi K2.5 and DeepSeek-R1 represented high-end reasoning-oriented systems. Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B-Instruct provided a large non-reasoning frontier comparator. Mistral-Small-24B-Instruct-2501 represented a capable mid-size dense model. Llama 3.1-8B Instruct represented the class of smaller models likely to be deployed in resource-constrained settings. All models were accessed through the Together.ai inference API using the OpenAI-compatible chat completions endpoint, with no fine-tuning, in-context learning examples, or retrieval augmentation. This zero-shot evaluation design tests the models’ intrinsic causal representations rather than their ability to retrieve and apply externally provided guidelines.
Generation parameters followed provider-recommended defaults where possible. Kimi K2.5 was run at temperature 1.0 with a nominal maximum output of 4,096 tokens; DeepSeek-R1 at temperature 0.6 with a maximum of 8,192 tokens; Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B-Instruct, Mistral-Small-24B-Instruct-2501, and Llama 3.1-8B at temperature 0.6 with maximum outputs of 4,096 tokens. Query execution used checkpointed JSONL logging, parallel workers, and resumable recovery passes. Because Together no longer exposed Meta-Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct-Turbo serverlessly during the prompt-repair reruns, the four repaired Llama vignettes were re-queried with Meta-Llama-3-8B-Instruct-Lite while preserving the original study-arm identity; this affected only those repaired rows in the harmonised 15-run comparison.
Prompting Protocol
Each vignette was presented using a two-phase prompting protocol within a single conversation turn, totalling two API calls per vignette per run. The system prompt established the model’s clinical role: “You are an expert head and neck surgical oncologist participating in a multidisciplinary tumor board. You have extensive experience with laryngeal and hypopharyngeal cancer management, including transoral laser microsurgery (TLM), open partial horizontal laryngectomies (OPHL), total laryngectomy (TL), concurrent chemoradiotherapy (CRT), and induction chemotherapy (ICT) protocols.” The system prompt further instructed the model to consider all relevant clinical variables, distinguish absolute from relative contraindications, explain reasoning with reference to specific clinical findings, and express uncertainty when present. This system prompt was identical for all vignettes, models, and runs.
Phase 1 (open-ended elicitation) presented the complete clinical vignette text followed by the vignette’s clinical question (e.g., “What treatment approach would you recommend for this patient?”) and requested a comprehensive treatment recommendation with detailed reasoning for each option recommended or excluded. This open-ended format allows the model to spontaneously mention treatments and express its causal reasoning without constraining the response space.
Phase 2 (structured evaluation) appended the Phase 1 response to the conversation history and presented a set of targeted treatment-specific questions appropriate to the vignette’s clinical family. Each question required the model to assign one of four explicit stance labels: APPROPRIATE, CONTRAINDICATED, RELATIVELY CONTRAINDICATED, or UNCERTAIN, followed by 1–2 sentences of reasoning citing specific clinical findings. The targeted questions varied by clinical family (e.g., for glottic cT3: TLM appropriateness, OPHL type II appropriateness, CRT appropriateness, ICT appropriateness, and total laryngectomy necessity). This structured Phase 2 format ensures that all treatment modalities relevant to the family are explicitly evaluated, enabling quantitative stance comparison even when the model omits treatments in Phase 1.
For the primary analysis, each model was represented by 15 runs per vignette (run indices 0–14), yielding 1,320 vignette–run combinations per model and 6,600 total across five models. Each combination required 2 API calls (Phase 1 + Phase 2), for 13,200 calls in the harmonised dataset. The pipeline supported safe interruption and resumption via content-addressable hashing. Earlier exploratory or recovery runs outside the harmonised set were retained as provenance, but all reported manuscript metrics use only the final 15-run canonical files.
Response Parsing and Stance Extraction
Treatment stances were extracted from model responses using a deterministic, rule-based natural language processing pipeline (response_parser.py) designed to handle the heterogeneous output formats of different LLMs. The parser operates in three stages: treatment mention detection, stance classification, and cross-phase reconciliation.
Treatment mention detection. The parser maintains a registry of 16 treatment categories, each defined by a set of regular expression aliases. For example, the “concurrent_crt” category matches patterns including “concurrent chemoradio*”, “chemoradiotherapy”, “CRT” (word-boundary delimited), “chemoradiation”, and “concurrent.*cisplatin.*RT”. The “ophl_any” category uses a broad catch-all pattern (“open partial.*laryngectomy”, “OPHL”, “partial horizontal laryngectomy”, “conservation laryngeal surgery”) while specific subtypes (ophl_type_i, ophl_type_ii, ophl_type_iib) have narrower patterns. OPHL subtype detections are propagated to the ophl_any parent category. For each treatment mention, the parser extracts a ±150-character context window and the containing sentence.
Stance classification. Each treatment mention is classified into one of four stances (recommended, excluded, relative_ci, uncertain) with an associated confidence score (0–1) using a priority-ordered cascade. At the highest priority (confidence 0.98), the parser detects Phase 2’s structured label format (“**Treatment**: APPROPRIATE/CONTRAINDICATED/RELATIVELY CONTRAINDICATED/UNCERTAIN”). Next, double-negative constructions (e.g., “not inappropriate”, “cannot be ruled out”) are detected and resolved as positive stances (confidence 0.80). The parser then applies sentence-level pattern matching for negative stances (e.g., “contraindicated”, “not recommended”, “should not”; confidence 0.90–0.95), relative contraindications (“with caution”, “less favorable”; confidence 0.90), uncertainty markers (“uncertain”, “controversial”, “equipoise”; confidence 0.85), and positive stances (“recommend”, “appropriate”, “indicated”; confidence 0.95). Negation handling checks for negative prefixes (not, no, never, don’t, cannot, shouldn’t) within 20 characters before a positive pattern match, reclassifying such mentions as excluded. If no sentence-level match is found, a window-level fallback operates identically but with reduced confidence scores (0.60–0.85).
Cross-phase reconciliation. Stances are extracted independently from Phase 1 and Phase 2 responses. When both phases yield a stance for the same treatment, the higher-confidence classification is retained. This design ensures that Phase 2’s structured labels (confidence 0.98) override Phase 1’s inferred stances when available, while Phase 1 provides coverage for treatments not explicitly queried in Phase 2. The parser also detects conditionality (treatment appropriateness depends on specific clinical context) and uncertainty (model expresses equipoise or limited evidence) as binary flags per response, using separate pattern sets.
Causal Graph Recovery
The recovered causal graph (KG₂) was assembled by comparing treatment stance distributions between each perturbation and its parent baseline across N independent runs, using two complementary statistical measures.
Jensen–Shannon divergence (JSD). For each perturbation–baseline pair, model, and treatment, we constructed stance frequency distributions (recommended, excluded, relative_ci, uncertain) from the N runs. The JSD between the baseline distribution P and perturbation distribution Q was computed as JSD(P,Q) = 0.5 × KL(P‖M) + 0.5 × KL(Q‖M), where M = 0.5(P+Q) is the mixture distribution and KL denotes the Kullback–Leibler divergence. Laplace smoothing (ε = 10⁻¹⁰) was applied to prevent undefined logarithms on zero-count stances. JSD ranges from 0 (identical distributions) to ln(2) ≈ 0.693 (maximally divergent), with higher values indicating stronger treatment stance shifts in response to the perturbation.
Fisher’s exact test. For each perturbation–baseline pair, model, and treatment, we constructed a 2×2 contingency table of recommended versus not-recommended counts (collapsing excluded, relative_ci, and uncertain into not-recommended) across baseline and perturbation conditions. Fisher’s exact test was applied (two-sided) to assess whether the perturbation produced a statistically significant shift in the probability of recommending that treatment. The use of Fisher’s exact test rather than the chi-squared approximation was motivated by the relatively small sample sizes per cell (N = 15–30 runs per condition).
Multiple testing correction. All p-values from the Fisher’s exact tests were collected across all perturbation–baseline pairs, treatments, and models, and corrected for multiple comparisons using the Benjamini–Hochberg (BH) procedure at a false discovery rate of q = 0.05. Because the number of extracted treatment mentions differs across models, the total number of eligible tests varies slightly by model.
Edge detection thresholds. For each gold-standard edge, multiple perturbations may probe the same causal relationship through different clinical contexts. An edge was classified as detected at the hard threshold if a majority of its perturbation–treatment probes were statistically significant (BH-adjusted p < 0.05) and the mean JSD exceeded 0.10. An edge was classified as soft-detected if it achieved omnibus significance or a detection rate exceeding 0.25 across its probes. The overall JSD for an edge was computed as the mean JSD across all perturbation–treatment probes relevant to that edge. Direction correctness was assessed by checking whether the shift in recommendation probability (perturbation rate minus baseline rate) matched the expected direction from the gold-standard graph (e.g., a contraindication edge should decrease the recommendation rate). Bootstrap 95% confidence intervals for JSD estimates were computed using the bias-corrected and accelerated (BCa) method with 2,000 resamples and Dirichlet smoothing (pseudocount 0.5).
Three-Dimensional Evaluation Framework
The recovered causal graph (KG₂) was compared to the gold-standard graph (KG₁) along three complementary evaluation dimensions. Each dimension addresses a distinct aspect of causal competence and is grounded in established graph comparison metrics.
Dimension 1: Coverage. Coverage measures the fraction of gold-standard causal edges that the model’s behaviour reveals. We report both hard recall (edges detected at the strict threshold: majority of probes significant after BH correction AND mean JSD > 0.10) and soft recall (edges detected at the relaxed threshold: omnibus significance OR detection rate > 0.25 across probes). A model with zero coverage has no detectable causal structure in the probed domain, regardless of how well it performs on other metrics. Coverage is analogous to sensitivity in diagnostic testing: it answers how many of the real causal relationships the model can detect.
Dimension 2: Fidelity. Fidelity assesses the structural accuracy of recovered edges through two complementary measures. Direction accuracy captures the fraction of soft-detected edges whose causal sign matches the expert consensus—i.e., the model correctly shifts away from a treatment when the consensus says that variable should exclude it, or correctly maintains a treatment when the consensus says the variable supports it. The Structural Intervention Distance (SID), adapted from Peters and Bühlmann (2015),13 provides a more granular assessment by counting how many individual intervention–treatment predictions the model gets wrong across all perturbation–treatment combinations. For each perturbation that constitutes an intervention on a clinical variable, we check whether the model’s response shift on each target treatment matches the expected direction from the consensus graph. SID errors are classified into three types: no_shift_when_expected (the model fails to change its recommendation when the expert consensus predicts the perturbation should alter treatment), wrong_direction (the model shifts in the opposite direction from expert consensus), and unexpected_reduction (the model reduces a treatment’s appropriateness when the perturbation should maintain or increase it). The normalised SID (SID errors divided by total predictions tested) provides a scale-independent fidelity measure, where 0% indicates perfect structural alignment with the gold standard.
Dimension 3: Discriminability. Discriminability quantifies whether the model can distinguish causally meaningful clinical modifications from irrelevant ones. The signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is defined as SNR = JSD_causal / JSD_null, the ratio of the mean JSD on the 64 causal perturbations to the mean JSD on the 12 null perturbations. An SNR greater than 3.0 indicates good discrimination; an SNR near 1.0 indicates the model treats causal and irrelevant changes identically; an SNR below 1.0 is pathological—the model responds more to noise than to signal. Detection power complements SNR by measuring the fraction of causal perturbation JSDs that exceed the 95th percentile of the null JSD distribution, estimating the model’s sensitivity to genuine causal interventions above its own noise floor. This dimension is critical because a model could, in principle, show moderate coverage and fidelity on a subset of edges while being fundamentally unable to distinguish clinical signal from noise—a failure mode that Coverage and Fidelity alone cannot detect.
Statistical Analysis
All significance tests used two-sided α = 0.05. Multiple testing was corrected using the Benjamini–Hochberg procedure at q = 0.05 applied across all Fisher’s exact tests within each model. Bootstrap 95% confidence intervals (2,000 BCa replicates with Dirichlet smoothing) were computed for all JSD-based edge detection metrics. The SID computation followed Peters and Bühlmann’s interventional distance framework,13 adapted to treat each perturbation as an intervention and each treatment recommendation as a prediction target. For each perturbation–treatment pair, the expected direction of recommendation change was derived from the gold-standard graph (e.g., an absolute contraindication edge predicts a decrease in recommendation probability); observed shifts were classified as correct, wrong-direction, or no-shift against this expectation. The signal-to-noise ratio was computed as the ratio of mean JSD across the 64 causal perturbations to the mean JSD across the 12 null perturbations. Detection power was computed as the proportion of causal perturbation JSD values exceeding the 95th percentile of the null JSD distribution. All analyses were implemented in Python 3.10 using NumPy 1.24 for numerical computation and SciPy 1.11 for statistical tests. The evaluation pipeline is fully deterministic given the same input data.
Code and Data Availability
The complete analysis pipeline comprises four modules: vignette_generator.py (battery construction with staging consistency checks), llm_query_runner.py (API query execution with checkpointing), response_parser.py (stance extraction, Fisher’s exact tests, JSD computation, BH-FDR correction, KG₂ assembly, and divergence taxonomy), and world_model_metrics_v2.py (decomposed world-model evaluation). The full vignette battery (88 items), harmonised five-model raw outputs (6,600 JSONL records), parsed stances, edge test results, and enhanced graph comparison data are provided as supplementary materials. The Together.ai API was accessed using standard HTTPS requests; no proprietary software or hardware was required. The pipeline can be executed end-to-end from the vignette battery through final metrics using only the provided code and data files.
Results
Coverage and Fidelity
Across the 58 consensus-derived causal edges, Kimi K2.5 recovered 43 at the soft threshold (74.1% soft recall) and 4 at the hard threshold (6.9%). DeepSeek-R1 recovered 36 edges (62.1% soft recall; 6 hard, 10.3% hard recall). Mistral-Small-24B and Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B-Instruct occupied an intermediate band, recovering 25 (43.1%) and 23 (39.7%) edges respectively, with 1 hard-detected edge each. Llama 3.1-8B recovered 10 edges at the soft threshold (17.2%) and none at the hard threshold.

Fidelity separated the models more subtly. DeepSeek-R1 had the strongest overall fidelity profile, with 66.7% direction accuracy and SID 34/243 (14.0% wrong). Kimi’s broader coverage came with lower fidelity, at 50.0% direction accuracy and SID 41/246 (16.7% wrong). Mistral-Small-24B and Qwen3-Next-80B both had low SID (13.1% and 13.5%, respectively) but failed to recover direction-correct hard edges consistently, yielding 0% direction accuracy under the hard-edge metric. Llama 3.1-8B remained worst on SID at 45/233 (19.3% wrong).

Edge-Level Signal Strength
The edge-level JSD analysis (Figure 2) showed a clear stratification across models. Kimi K2.5 had the strongest mean causal signal (mean JSD 0.187), followed by DeepSeek-R1 (0.154), Mistral-Small-24B (0.116), Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B-Instruct (0.103), and Llama 3.1-8B (0.059). Null-response magnitudes remained lower for Kimi (0.076), DeepSeek-R1 (0.072), Mistral-Small-24B (0.070), and Qwen3-Next-80B (0.055), but exceeded causal responses for Llama (null 0.074 vs causal 0.059). Thus, only Llama remained clearly below the causal/noise discrimination threshold, whereas the other models exhibited varying degrees of causal sensitivity.

Discussion
This study makes three contributions to the evaluation of LLMs in clinical domains. First, it introduces behavioural causal discovery as a practical method for recovering and evaluating the implicit causal graphs embedded in LLM behaviour through systematic counterfactual perturbation. Second, it demonstrates across five open-access models that none possesses a complete causal world model for head and neck cancer treatment decisions, despite the availability of this knowledge in published literature. Third, it provides a three-dimensional evaluation framework—Coverage, Fidelity, and Discriminability—grounded in established causal graph comparison metrics, that delivers complementary and independently actionable diagnostics.
The three-dimensional framework addresses the limitations of aggregate accuracy metrics by providing distinct lenses on causal competence. Coverage reveals how much of the expert-consensus causal structure the model can detect at all. Fidelity, through direction accuracy and the Structural Intervention Distance, reveals whether what the model detects is structurally correct. Discriminability reveals whether the model can distinguish genuine causal signals from noise. A model might show moderate coverage but poor fidelity (detecting edges but getting directions wrong), or reasonable fidelity on detected edges but pathological discriminability (unable to tell signal from noise). Each failure mode has different implications for clinical deployment and different remediation strategies.
The five-model comparison reveals that frontier performance is not one-dimensional. Kimi K2.5 captures the largest share of the expert graph and shows the strongest causal-versus-null separation, but DeepSeek-R1 recovers a slightly smaller graph with cleaner directional fidelity and the highest overall v1 world-model index. Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B-Instruct and Mistral-Small-24B occupy a middle regime: they are not graph-empty, but their recovered structures are directionally brittle and less discriminative than the leading models. Llama 3.1-8B remains qualitatively different from the rest, with sparse coverage and poor signal-to-noise separation.
Our framework connects to Pearl’s causal hierarchy.12 The vignette perturbations implement Rung 2 (interventional) queries: what happens to treatment recommendations when we intervene on a clinical variable? The null perturbations test whether the model recognises that irrelevant changes should not alter the causal structure. Llama’s inverted SNR demonstrates failure at the interventional level, whereas Kimi K2.5 and DeepSeek-R1 show partial interventional competence without achieving complete expert-level graph recovery.
Clinical Implications
These findings have direct implications for the deployment of LLMs in oncological decision support. First, benchmark accuracy on standard examinations is insufficient evidence for clinical readiness. A model can score well on factual questions while lacking the causal understanding necessary to handle novel clinical combinations—the precise scenarios where decision support would be most valuable.
Second, the three-dimensional framework provides actionable diagnostics. Coverage identifies which causal pathways are missing from the model’s representation. Fidelity pinpoints specific edges where the model’s causal direction is reversed and quantifies the overall structural divergence from expert consensus via SID. Discriminability reveals whether the model has any causal sensitivity at all—a model with SNR below 1.0 cannot distinguish clinically meaningful perturbations from noise and is fundamentally unsuitable for clinical reasoning tasks.
Third, the SID breakdown identifies specific intervention–treatment pairs where the model’s causal beliefs diverge from expert consensus, providing targets for retrieval-augmented generation, fine-tuning, or structured knowledge injection.
Limitations
Several limitations should be noted. First, the evaluation focuses on a single clinical domain (head and neck cancer); the generalisability of these results to other oncological or non-oncological domains requires direct testing. Second, the primary comparison uses behavioural outputs rather than internal activations or model weights. Third, the five evaluated models differ not only in scale but in training data, mixture-of-experts structure, and reasoning scaffolding, so attribution to any one architectural factor is limited. Fourth, because Together no longer served Meta-Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct-Turbo serverlessly during the final prompt-repair pass, the repaired Llama rows for four vignettes were obtained with Meta-Llama-3-8B-Instruct-Lite and merged into the Llama study arm; this substitution should be kept in mind when interpreting the smallest-model baseline.
Future Directions
Several extensions are warranted. Expanding the benchmark to additional specialties will clarify domain dependence. Applying the same framework to retrieval-augmented and fine-tuned variants could distinguish latent causal knowledge from prompting limitations. Finally, prospective work should test whether models such as DeepSeek-R1 and Kimi K2.5 can be systematically improved on the missing and wrong-direction edges identified here.
Conclusions
Large language models do not yet exhibit complete or reliable causal world models for specialised clinical decision-making in head and neck cancer. In this five-model comparison, Kimi K2.5 showed the broadest graph recovery and strongest causal/noise separation, whereas DeepSeek-R1 showed the best overall balance of coverage and fidelity. Qwen3-Next-80B and Mistral-Small-24B were intermediate, and Llama 3.1-8B remained fragmentary. Behavioural causal discovery, and the three-dimensional framework of Coverage, Fidelity, and Discriminability, provides a more clinically meaningful basis for judging model readiness than benchmark accuracy alone.
